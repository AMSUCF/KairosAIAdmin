AI ADMIN

Provocations through Generated Play
Anastasia Salter

Project Overview

This interactive project explores the complexities of artificial intelligence in academic administration through two playable games that examine how generative AI tools reshape both writing program administration and digital humanities center leadership. Created primarily through iterative dialogues with large language models, the games themselves embody the collaborative human-AI authorship they critique.

Each version presents players with scenarios that highlight the tensions between efficiency and ethics, innovation and exploitation, speed and sustainability. The games ask: What happens when administrative decisions become algorithmic? How do we navigate the environmental costs of computational convenience? What gets lost when human judgment is outsourced to pattern recognition?

Rather than offering simple answers, these "provocations through generated play" invite reflection on our evolving relationship with AI tools in higher education. The retro aesthetic and deliberate inefficiencies of the code serve as counterpoints to the sleek promises of AI optimization, suggesting that some forms of resistance might be found in embracing the human, the slow, and the intentionally imperfect.

On Generated Authorship

These games were created through approximately 200 iterative prompts across multiple AI systems (ChatGPT 4.0, Claude Opus 4.0, Claude Sonnet 4.0, DALL-E 3), with human curation, editing, and hand-coding throughout the process. The resulting artifacts raise questions about agency, creativity, and labor in an age of computational collaboration.

The code is intentionally inefficient—over 1,100 lines of JavaScript for relatively simple interactions—serving as a meditation on optimization culture. The visual assets, generated through AI image tools and then extensively modified, reflect a deliberate move away from photorealism toward the aesthetic possibilities of computational creativity.

For scholars in rhetoric, composition, and digital humanities, these games offer both a critical lens and a creative methodology. They suggest that our engagement with AI tools might be most productive when it remains reflexive, experimental, and willing to embrace the productive tensions between human and machine authorship.
